1st & 2nd Generation (1970s)

Magnavox Odyssey

The Magnavox Odyssey was the first commercial home video game console and relied entirely on analog circuitry rather than a programmable processor.

Its games were implemented through hardware configurations and physical screen overlays, with no built-in scoring or sound.

Atari

First-generation arcade games, such as Pong, introduced video games to the public with simple rules and accessible gameplay.

The second-generation console Atari 2600 brought gaming into homes, popularizing cartridge-based systems and expanding the game market.

Atari Shock (1983-1985)

Atari Shock

The Atari Shock, also known as the video game crash of 1983, was caused by market saturation, poor-quality games, and a lack of quality control in the console and software industry.

Consumer trust declined rapidly as too many low-quality titles (ex. ET, Pacman) flooded the market, leading to sharp drops in sales and company bankruptcies.

This collapse prompted the industry to adopt stricter licensing and quality control systems in later generations.

3rd Generation(mid 1980s, 8-bit era)

Family Controller(Femi-com)

Released in 1983, the Family Controller helped revive the video game industry after the market crash.

Its cartridge-based system and strict quality control set standards for home consoles.

Family Controller introduced iconic franchises such as Super Mario Bros. and The Legend of Zelda.

After the Atari Shock in 1983, the console gaming industry experienced a severe decline, with many companies exiting the market due to oversupply and poor-quality games.

The industry remained unstable for several years until Nintendo released the Family Computer (Famicom), which reintroduced quality control and reliable game publishing practices.

With the success of the Famicom(from 1985), consumer trust was gradually restored, leading to the recovery and long-term growth of the home console market.

4th Generation (late 1980s~, 16-bit era)

SNES(aka Super Femi-com)

SNES(Super Nintendo Entertainment System) Released in the early 1990s, the Super Famicom became one of the most iconic consoles of the 4th generation.

With advanced colors and high-quality sound than Family Controller, a wide range of memorable games such as Super Mario World and early Final Fantasy series.

The console helped define home gaming as a family-friendly and long-lasting entertainment medium.

Sega Genesis

Sega Genesis released in 1988 which was a major 4th generation console that focused on fast gameplay and arcade-style action.

Powered by a higher-clocked CPU, it delivered arcade-style gameplay and smoother action in many titles, including Sonic the Hedgehog.

Its aggressive marketing and emphasis on speed made it a key player in the famous “console wars” of the era.

SNES vs Sega Genesis : First Console War

Technically, the Sega Genesis featured a significantly faster CPU, making it well-suited for fast-paced and arcade-style games, while the Super Famicom excelled in graphics and sound through advanced color support and dedicated audio hardware.

Beyond raw specifications, the Super Famicom consistently held an advantage in game quality, supported by a stronger lineup of first-party titles and more polished game design.

As a result, Sega always positioned the Genesis as challenger, highlighting speed and attitude in contrast to Nintendo’s broader technical and content-driven dominance during the 4th generation console era.

5th Generation (mid 1990s~, 3D era)

PlayStation

PlayStation was released in 1994 and marked Sony’s entry into the video game console market.

By adopting CD-ROM technology, it enabled larger, more complex 3D games and attracted many third-party developers.

Its strong software support and focus on 3D gaming helped redefine the industry and establish Sony as a major player.

Nintendo64

Nintendo 64 was released in 1996, following the success of the PlayStation and further advancing 3D console gaming.

It introduced groundbreaking 3D gameplay experiences with titles like Super Mario 64, setting new standards for camera control and level design.

Although it relied on cartridges instead of CDs, the Nintendo 64 demonstrated Nintendo’s unique approach to 3D gaming and innovation.

Nintendo64 vs PlayStation : Second Console War

By examining both best-selling game titles and overall console sales, the outcome of the second console war becomes clear.

While the Nintendo 64 produced a smaller number of highly successful games, the PlayStation benefited from a broader and more diverse library, resulting in significantly higher total console sales worldwide.

Together, these factors establish the PlayStation as the clear winner of the second console war.
